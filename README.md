# Ravel

### A Game of Threads by Mostly Fuzz Studio

---

## What the TRPG Experience Should Be

TRPGs bring something no other medium can: the possibility of an open-ended simulation of characters and world. Video games can't do this because they're bounded by what's programmed. Fiction can't because it's not interactive. Improv can't because there's no persistent world-state or consistent adjudication.

But simulation is a means, not the end. We don't want every mundane moment processed. What we actually want is **consequential choice in a coherent world**. The simulation matters only insofar as it makes your choices *real*. If the world doesn't respond consistently, your decisions are meaningless. But if the simulation includes how well you digest breakfast, that's noise, not meaning.

The soap opera principle: you see the mundane only when something interesting can happen. There is always intent in the scene. Thursday's breakfast exists because the marriage cracks open there. Tuesday's breakfast doesn't exist at all.

The core TRPG design challenge isn't "how do we simulate the world" but: **how do we reliably generate situations where choices feel consequential, and then adjudicate those choices honestly?**

## What Fiasco Gets Right

Fiasco's structure guarantees you're telling a story about people and their entanglements. You can't accidentally spend three sessions clearing a dungeon that has nothing to do with anyone's actual stakes. The structure forces character-centricity.

The rhythm is right—scenes, spotlight rotation, the Tilt as a midpoint pivot. It creates the *pacing* of curated consequential moments. You're never playing Tuesday breakfast. You're always at Thursday's table.

**What Fiasco gets right:**
- No option but a story about characters and their connections
- The web of connections contains threads from the very start
- Episodic scenes with passed-around spotlight
- The Tilt creates natural escalation
- In a high-trust table, it reliably creates interesting stories

## What Fiasco Lacks

Fiasco runs on narrative logic, not world logic. The dice don't adjudicate your choices—they determine whether your ending is good or bad based on a pool accumulated, divorced from the specifics of what you did. The causality is aesthetic, not logical.

**The coherence is entirely honor-system.** The game trusts you to remember that your character is desperate, that this relationship is strained, that this object matters. If you play against the grain, nothing pushes back. No mechanism says "wait, that doesn't follow."

Fiasco nails shape. Ignores coherence.
Traditional games attempt coherence. Often fumble shape.

## The Ravel Engine

### The Core Insight

You don't need rules for the world. You need rules for *the scene*. The scene is the unit of play, and coherence only has to hold there. What's been established about this character, this relationship, this situation becomes mechanically binding within the scene.

### The Economy: Honor → Dice → Pull

**Earning Dice**

During a scene, when you exercise your character's drive, logical approach, or anything connected to established relationships, needs, or fiction—you earn a die. The mechanism:

1. You **point** to the established element you're honoring ("I'm playing Maria's desperation here")
2. You **deliver** — you play it in the scene
3. The **table validates** — they confirm your delivery honored the element
4. You **earn a die**

This creates:
- **Legibility** — everyone knows what's being claimed
- **Intentionality** — you consciously connect choices to the established web
- **Gentle accountability** — the table can say no
- **Teaching** — players internalize what "honoring an element" looks like

**Pulling Threads**

The web of connections, relationships, needs, and established fiction contains threads—questions, tensions, connections that could reveal something. When you want to pull on a thread, you spend earned dice.

The three outcomes:

- **Clarity** — the thread resolves into established fiction. You now know *why*. That knowledge becomes load-bearing going forward.
- **Cold** — the thread doesn't resolve, but its *resistance* becomes established fiction. Something is missing, hidden, inaccessible. The absence itself is now real. "There are no files on Sonia from 1998. Why is that?" Cold is not "nothing happens" — it's generative.
- **Breaks and Hurts** — pulling on this made something worse. The thread snaps and the tension releases in a bad direction. Active damage.

There is no wasted roll. Every outcome gives material.

### The Loop

Honor the established fiction → earn dice → pull threads → new established fiction enters play → which you must then honor.

**Coherence feeds discovery feeds coherence.**

## The GM: A Player with a Different Portfolio

Ravel has a GM-like figure, but fundamentally reimagined. Not the world-author. Not the adversary. A **steward of emergence** whose job is to catch the things that would otherwise fall, give voice to elements that need a voice, and deliver consequences when the dice demand them.

**What the GM does:**
- Plays NPCs in scenes, giving them interiority and coherence
- Facilitates consequences ("breaks and hurts" has a home)
- Watches the whole web, noticing neglected threads
- Frames scenes toward dangling potential
- Discovers alongside everyone else

**The critical principle: the GM plays by the same economy.**

The GM earns dice by honoring established NPC elements, pointing to them, having the table validate. The GM pulls threads and spends dice. When the GM pulls, the *table delivers the outcome*. This means:

- **The GM can't plant.** They can't seed mysteries they already know the answer to.
- **Players get authorship over the world.** When the GM pulls and gets clarity, players say what happened.
- **Everyone is vulnerable.** The GM puts NPCs they care about at risk.
- **NPCs become real.** The GM is mechanically bound to honor what's established about them. The villain can't monologue when coherence demands he'd just shoot.

The GM/player hierarchy is eliminated and replaced with a **division of labor inside a shared economy**. Same engine, same rules. The GM just has a different portfolio: NPCs, world-texture, facilitation.

## Campaigns: The Web That Keeps Evolving

Ravel supports campaign play because threads are the persistence layer. Unlike Fiasco, which demands combustion and resolution, Ravel's threads can go cold, transform, and accumulate across sessions.

### What Persists Between Sessions
- The relationship map (which grows, shifts, loses and gains nodes)
- Established fiction (everything given clarity)
- Cold threads (unanswered questions waiting)
- Character texture (accumulated through honoring and revealing)

### What Changes
- New threads emerge from play
- Relationships shift based on events
- Characters enter and exit
- The questions the table is curious about evolve

### Thread Timescales

**Short threads** — the episode's focus. They emerge, get pulled, resolve or break within the session. Immediate texture.

**Mid threads** — the season arc. They surface across episodes, get pulled occasionally, accumulate clarity or damage. The "why do Carlos and Sonia hate each other" that takes four sessions to excavate.

**Long threads** — character-level. Who *is* Maria? What does her desperation cost her over time? These accrue through play, through all the times you honored an element and what that did.

### Session Structure

Each session is episodic—complete in itself with shape and crescendo—but bolts onto the next through persistent threads. The TV series model. Each episode stands alone, but you tune in next week because characters are still moving and long threads are still dangling.

End each session with: which threads are hot, which went cold, what new threads exist, what broke? That's your campaign state.

## Character Development

No XP. No advancement tracks. No "pick a new ability."

Character development is the narrative consequence of sustained play, formalized at natural breakpoints.

### How It Works

Between episodes, look at what you've been honoring. Maria's desperation has been load-bearing for three sessions. You've pointed to it a dozen times. Things have happened around it—she lied to her sister, took the job she shouldn't have, let Peter down.

Now, as Maria's player, you **author the shift**. Given everything that's happened, what has this become? Not whatever you want—what the fiction *implies*. The established events constrain you. If Maria's desperation led her to betray people, she doesn't get to emerge kinder.

But you choose the specific shape: harder, numb, more cunning, more ashamed.

### Properties of Character Development in Ravel
- **Emergent** — from actual play, not predetermined
- **Authored** — by the player, with table validation
- **Constrained** — by established fiction
- **Mechanically integrated** — the new element enters the economy

The new element replaces what came before. You don't get dice for playing old-Maria's desperation anymore. You have to play what she's become.

Playing the game well naturally progresses your character. The same engine at a different timescale.

## Combat: Just Another Scene

TRPGs have had it backwards from the beginning. D&D descended from wargames—combat got the crunch because that's what the inherited chassis knew how to do. But look at the fiction TRPGs claim to emulate: Firefly, Breaking Bad, The Wire. 90% or more is dialogue. Battles are punctuation, not the text.

**Ravel does not have combat mechanics.** A fight is just another scene, resolved by the same engine.

Being a skilled fighter is established fiction. It's something you honor. Peter's military background means when violence happens, he knows what he's doing—you point to that, the table validates, you earn a die.

But the die doesn't make you win. It lets you pull threads. The fight resolves through accumulation of established fiction and consequences of thread-pulling. Someone gets hurt because that's what clarity or a break demanded, not because dice said 17 damage.

This means fights are *expensive*—not mechanically, but fictionally. Violence matters because it changes things, reveals things, frays things. It can't be routine because routine produces no threads worth pulling.

## The Name

**Ravel** is a contranym. It means both to untangle *and* to entangle. To make clearer and to make more knotted. Simultaneously.

This is exactly what happens in the game. You pull a thread for clarity, and the clarity creates new complications. The fiction becomes more understood and more tangled at the same time. Every answer generates new questions. Every revealed truth complicates something else.

Fray implied an endpoint—everything wears down, eventually it's gone. Entropy.

Ravel is ongoing. Every untangling is also a tangling. There's no final state. Just deeper and deeper into the weave.

### Visual Identity

White cover. Tall, thin, fragile black letters. A single thread—but is it coming loose or being woven in? You can't tell. That's the point. The thread is barely visible, almost disappearing into the white, only its imperfection—its fuzz—making it real.

## Summary: The Rules on an Index Card

1. **Setup:** Fiasco-style relationship map with connections, needs, and initial threads
2. **Scenes:** Spotlight rotates. Scenes are framed around potential—curated consequential moments
3. **Honor:** When you play to an established element, point to it. If the table accepts, earn a die
4. **Pull:** Spend dice to pull on a thread
5. **Outcomes:** Clarity (the fiction expands) / Cold (the absence is real) / Breaks (something gets worse)
6. **Delivery:** Whoever doesn't own the thread narrates the outcome. When GM pulls, the table answers. When players pull, anyone can deliver (often the GM)
7. **Everyone plays by the same rules.** GM earns dice and pulls threads like everyone else
8. **Between episodes:** Evolve your character based on what play has established. New element enters the economy
9. **Campaign:** Threads persist. The web transforms. Play continues

Teach in five minutes. The depth comes from play, not from rules.
