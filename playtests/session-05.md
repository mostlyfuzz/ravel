## Act One

### Scene 1 — Daal and Fen (nav room)

Fen came to the nav room without announcement. First time she'd been there since before the 45th response. Stopped in the doorway when she saw the web.

Daal didn't turn around. Long silence. Fen: "It's bigger than I thought."

Fen named it in thirty seconds: the web's geometry matches the transmission. Same branching interval, same nodal clusters. Six hours of staring and Daal hadn't seen it. She had it before she reached the viewport.

Daal: "My mother built navigation arrays. She taught me the geometry. The web looks like her work."

Fen: "Is she alive?"

Daal didn't answer.

[Daal honored Drive: to understand. Pool: 3. Fen honored Drive: curiosity as wound — she came despite herself. Pool: 3.]

New thread: **The transmission and the web share a geometry** — same structure at different scales. Whoever built the transmission understood the web intimately. Jun? Daal's mother? Something older?

New branch off **How did Daal's mother have the coordinates?** → **Is Daal's mother alive — and does she know the Harrow arrived?**

### Scene 2 — Maren and Fen (med bay)

Fen brought the geometry discovery to Maren. Lian asleep beside them.

Maren mapped it as a system: the transmission traveled toward them, they traveled to the web, same geometry, opposite directions. "We're not outside receiving a signal anymore. We're nodes." Then: "Fen, our receivers are pointing outward — could we point them inward? If we're nodes, we must be part of the message going out."

Fen went still. Watched Maren the way she used to watch the transmitter. "That's what I thought. When I found the signal. I thought I was pointing the receiver. Forty-three times I thought that."

Maren's cleverness is real — the logic holds. Fen is the only one who knows what it costs to be right about this.

[Maren honored Approach: Meticulous. Pool: 4. Fen honored relationship with Lian — she's protecting the table from her own mistake. Pool: 3.]

Thread evolved: **Is this contact or consumption?** → **Are we the message?**
The crew didn't receive a transmission. They may be a transmission, addressed to something that was waiting.

### Scene 3 — Lian's Dream (med bay)

Lian fell asleep in her data. In the dream: the cube, but translucent — the web visible beyond the walls. She's always been seeing it from the inside.

The shape was there. She moved toward it. Closer than she's ever gotten. She reached out and touched its shoulder.

A hand came from the shadows and grabbed hers before contact. Too strong to pull away. The shape phased in and out — hologram-like, but shivering, as if the pull caused pain it didn't know how to express. It stiffened. Pulled her toward it. More appendages. Her whole body drawn closer, face approaching the back of the shape's head, about to be swallowed.

At the threshold — the widest, whitest eyes appeared in the back of the shape's head.

*Manifestation.* Lian's peripheral vision disappeared. Before she passed out in terror, one thought: **Not Jun.** The shape: *"I am there."*

She woke up drenched in cold sweat.

**Note:** "Not Jun" is Lian's perception in a moment of terror, not established fact. It may be true. It may be what the shape wanted her to believe.

Thread evolved: **Jun won't turn around** → **Who has Lian been reaching toward — and was it ever Jun?**
Three episodes of Cold. The shape finally responded. In the moment before waking, Lian thought: *not Jun.* The shape said: *I am there.* Present tense. Already arrived. Already somewhere.
*Cold ×3 — evolved*

## Design Findings

### 1. Thread Format as Orientation Tool

The richer thread format (story anchor, not label) meant a player away for weeks could orient quickly from the thread list alone without needing a separate summary. The thread body should carry enough context to answer: what is the question, what do we know, what makes it heavy.

### 2. Manifestation as Gift

Lian's dream happened because the GM narrated her falling asleep and mentioned the shape. That framing opened a space the player took. The manifestation was organic — not "now manifest," but "here is an opening." The GM's job includes putting things down for players to pick up. That's not just yes-and, it's active setup.

### 3. The Yes-And Dynamic Is the Engine

Character ownership was loose — both players shaped all characters throughout. What drove the session was the back-and-forth: one player puts something down, the other picks it up and takes it somewhere. That exchange is more important than who owns which character.

### 4. Bomb Scene Needs Escalation

The bomb scene was framed too lightly this session — stated rather than played. Next session: multiple characters present, layers of escalation building to the crack. The break should feel inevitable in retrospect.
